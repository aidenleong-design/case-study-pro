# How Design Leaders Read Portfolios

Calibration context, not the primary lens. A case study should present how
someone works; done well, it naturally covers what reviewers look for.

## The five dimensions reviewers assess

1. **Problem-solving & judgment** — Understood the problem deeply, or just
   executed a brief? Showed constraints and how they shaped decisions? Explored
   multiple directions? Can articulate why rejected approaches were killed?
2. **Craft & execution** — Visual: beautiful, systematic, intentional. UX: clear
   flows, sound hierarchy. Design engineer: shipped product matches intent,
   details polished. All: the case study itself shows care.
3. **Impact & outcomes** — Specific and believable ("67% to 41%," not "improved
   20%"). Qualitative impact grounded in evidence. Confounders acknowledged. For
   unshipped work, proxy outcomes (usability tests, decisions influenced).
4. **Strategic & systems thinking** — Considered the broader ecosystem,
   negotiated scope, thought about scalability, edge cases, accessibility.
5. **Point of view** — Has opinions about design. You can tell what they believe.
   Feels authored, not assembled. There's a voice.

## Patterns that weaken a portfolio

1. No thinking shown — only final mockups.
2. No outcomes or reflection.
3. Vague individual contribution ("we did…").
4. Every case study reads the same (the "case study factory").
5. Pretty visuals, weak narrative.
6. Overclaimed credit.
7. Fabricated metrics (suspiciously round, no baselines).
8. No accessibility considerations — table stakes now.
9. No personality — could have been written by anyone, or an AI.

## What makes a portfolio memorable

- **Problem reframing** — solved a better problem than the one given. Push: "Was
  the problem you solved the same one you were handed?"
- **Visible struggle** — the messy middle and killed directions. Push: "What
  didn't work? What was the hard part?"
- **Honest outcomes** — real numbers with baselines, or honest "it didn't ship
  because leadership pivoted." Push: "Do you have real evidence? If not, let's be
  honest about it."
- **Genuine reflection** — evidence they learn and carry it forward. "What would
  you do differently?" is the simple version; "How did this change how you think
  about design?" is the deeper one.

## Company-culture context (only if targeting a specific role)

- **Google** — end-to-end thinking, data-informed, collaboration evidence.
- **Apple** — craft and restraint, editorial judgment about what to remove.
- **Stripe** — minimal, strategy-forward, business framing first.
- **Meta** — clearly articulated problems, explorations, final walkthrough.
- **Linear / Vercel / Figma** — shipped product as portfolio, craft-essay format,
  link to live work.
- **Startups** — speed, scrappiness, multiple hats, shipping under constraints.
